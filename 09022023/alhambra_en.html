History of the Alhambra

The Alhambra takes place on the Sabika hill during the IXth century. He is a former prime minister named Samuel ibn Nagrella who rebuilt the abandoned buildings on this hill and installed his palace there. and installed his palace there.

In the XIIth century, These buildings are used as a refuge during fights in Alcazaba del Albaicín.

In 1238, the founder of the Nasrid dynasty, Al-Ahmar, settled in the old Alcazaba of the Albaicín. He decided to rebuild the ruins of the hill, thus building the current Alhambra. It was a palace, a citadel and a fortress, but above all, a residence of the Nasrid high officials. Granada became the capital of the Nasrid kingdom, growing over time to make room for new neighborhoods until the end of the fifteenth century.

After 1492, the Alhambra became a Royal House.

Emperor Charles V decides, in 1526, to build his eponymous palace, as well as other buildings having for style the Roman Renaissance.

In the XIXth century, the company is very interested in the gardens of the Alhambra. Following the revolution of 1868, the Alhambra is detached from the Crown and becomes the domain of the State, being declared in 1870 "national monument".
Currently, the custody of the Alhambra is entrusted to the Autonomous Community Andalusia of the functions and services of the State in the field of culture.


Arts & Architectures

It was in the 14th century that the Alhambra was embellished with decorations with multiple Muslim symbols, notably by Yusuf I. The justice of Granada was administered in the Puerta de la Ley Islamica, which makes the Alhambra more than just a defensive or decorative building. Its defensive architecture makes the Alhambra a fortress (ramparts, fortified gates, guard towers) with an "oriental" aesthetic that was used by the Emirs who resided in the Generalife, located in the Alhamlbra.
It is noticeable that the last buildings have many round arches and domed roofs, to let in as much light as possible, an important theme in Muslim architecture.

Granada corresponds to the historical model of a city-hill, surrounded by mountains and watered by the rivers Darro and Genil. Surrounded by mountains, Arab writers have compared Granada to a crown, on the front of which stands the "diadem of the Alhambra".




Monuments within the Alhambra

The Nasrid Palace

Mexuar

The Mexuar is located in the northern part of the Alhambra and is accessible through a large patio called the Patio de los Arrayanes.

The Mexuar was used as an audience hall and a meeting place for government business. It was used by Muslim rulers to hold audiences and to discuss the affairs of the kingdom with their advisors.

Palais Comares

The Comares Palace is the main palace of the Alhambra.

The Comares Palace is known for its exceptional architecture and luxurious decorations, which reflect the wealth and power of the Nasrid dynasty.

The palace also includes a large audience hall called the Hall of Ambassadors, which is decorated with marble columns and ceramic mosaics.

Palais des Lions

It is one of the most famous buildings of the Alhambra and is known for its central fountain fountain in the shape of a lion, hence its name.

The palace consists of several rooms, each decorated with geometric patterns and colorful frescoes. The Lion Palace is also known for its lush gardens and fountains.

The Palaces

    Alcabaza

The Alcazaba is the oldest part of the Alhambra and was built in the 11th century. The Alcazaba was originally a fortress designed to protect the city of Granada from enemy invasions. It was completed by Muhammad I who also built the Keep and the Torre de Vela..

The building is divided into two parts: the upper part, called the citadel, was used as a residence for the Moorish rulers and their court, while the lower part was used the lower part was used as a military quarter.

The Alcazaba has been repaired several times over the centuries, especially by the Christians, and has been used as a state prison at different times, including during the Franco occupation.

    Palace of Charles V

The Palace of Carlos V is a palace built on the orders of the emperor Charles V, who had made the Alhambra his summer residence. Although it began construction in 1527, the palace was completed in 1957, due to lack of funds or collapses.

The palace is square and cuts across the gardens connecting the entrance of the Alhambra to the Alcazaba, since the palace is located next to the Nasrid Palaces.

Only the south and west sides of the building are fully decorated, while the north and east facades are only partially decorated, as the building was once connected to the Alcazaba of the Alhambra.

    The Baths

The Alhambra Baths include two baths, one for men and one for women. Each of them is composed of several rooms, including a hot or steam room "Caldarium", a Caldarium", a cold bath "Frigidarium", a main room "Tepidarium" and a massage room and a massage room and a relaxation room. The baths were also equipped with fountains and basins for hot and cold water.

The decoration of the Baths of the Alhambra is rich and sophisticated, with many details such as mosaics, geometric patterns, arabesques and inscriptions in Arabic. in Arabic. The baths were also equipped with oil lamps and large glass mirrors to diffuse natural light.

    Generalife

The Generalife is a garden located on the slopes of the Sun Hill in Granada, from where you can see the whole city and the the city and the valleys of the rivers Genil and Darro. It was used as a place of relaxation for the kings of Granada when they wanted to escape the official life in the palace.


The Courtyards & Gardens

Garden of the Partal

Its name refers to a portico, since it connects a myriad of buildings of the Alhambra (It is surrounded by important buildings of the Alhambra, such as the Comares Palace and the Palace of the Leones). The garden of the Partal is known for its fountains and pools, which were designed to cool the air and create a pleasant atmosphere for visitors. There are also many plants and trees in the garden, including olive trees, cypresses and orange trees. It overlooks the Torre de la Dama,a building known for housing merchants and tycoons of the who came to relax and greet the royalty.

Cour des Myrtes

The current name of this place is the Courtyard of the Myrtles because of the presence of myrtle bushes around the central pond and the bright green color that contrasts with the white marble floor of the patio. The Courtyard of the Myrtles is a long courtyard that surrounds the Alberca Pond. The rooms on the sides were used as a residence for women. In the north gallery, we can read a poem by Ibn Zamrak in honor of Muhammad V after the conquest of Algeciras in 1368.

Cour des Lions

The courtyard of the Lions is the most famous place in the Alhambra. The fountain in the middle of the patio, which is composed of twelve lions-jets of water and rests on a large dodecagonal basin in white marble, gave its name to the complex. This fountain is considered one of the most important examples of Muslim sculpture. The Gallery of the Court of Lions is a covered space supported by 124 white marble columns. It was listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1984.
